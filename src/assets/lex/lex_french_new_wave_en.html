French New Wave (French: La Nouvelle Vague): A film movement of the late 1950s and 1960s, which largely originated among young French film critics, who turned to filmmaking as a reaction against the traditional French film industry, which in their opinion, was not spontaneous and adventurous enough.
Some of the most prominent pioneers among the group, which included François Truffaut, Jean-Luc Godard, Éric Rohmer, Claude Chabrol, and Jacques Rivette, began as critics for the famous Paris-based film magazine Cahiers du Cinéma. André Bazin, the co-founder of the magazine and film fellow theorist, was a prominent voice and source of influence for the movement.
