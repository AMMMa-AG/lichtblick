sound and music: In almost every film, the aspect of creating sound effects and sound design accounts for a huge part of the production and post-production process. Generally, a differentiation can be made between diegetic and non-diegetic sounds. In films, all of the aspects that the characters themselves experience and encounter and that belong to the fictional world of the film, such as spoken words, an explosion, or even the live music which is played in a bar while the actors have a talk at the counter, are considered to be parts of its diegetic world. In contrast, all of the auditory aspects that the audience can conceive, but that do not belong to the actual ongoing action, are classified as being part of the non-diegetic world of a film, for example, a voice-over-narration or a song title that is only audible for the audience to create a specific mood or atmosphere.

Diegetic sounds can further be divided into:
  • Ambient sounds: A sound or collection of sounds that is apparently coming from within the film’s world, such as the sound of the wind, the rustling of leaves, noises produced by a crowd of people, or traffic noises, etc.
  • Onscreen sounds: All sounds emitted by sources that are visible within the frame.
  • Offscreen sounds: All sounds emitted by sources that are not visible within the frame, but that nevertheless belong to the diegetic world of a film.

In addition to these categories, there are also:
  • Sound effects: Any sound that is not part of the music or the dialogue, such as footsteps or a police siren.
  • Soundtrack: Everything audible in the film, including the film score, all sound effects and dialogues.
  • Film score: The musical part of a soundtrack, which can be part of the diegetic world of a film. However, because the film score also includes every title that is added to the film in order to influence the atmosphere, it also typically includes music that can be referred to as being non-diegetic.
