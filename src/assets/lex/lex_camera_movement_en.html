camera movement: There are three main types of camera movement and each of these can be divided into further categories:
  1. Movements with a camera mounted on a stationary tripod (fixed camera)
    Panning: The camera moves along its horizontal axis, from right to left or vice versa. A pan rapid enough to blur the image is referred to as a swish pan.
    Tilting: The camera pivots up or down along its vertical axis, thus changing the camera angle.
    Rotate: The camera rotates around an imaginary axis that runs between the lens and the subject.
  2. The moving camera (mobile camera)
    Tracking shot: The camera, which is mounted on a mobile support (e.g. a dolly) and typically guided by tracks (rails), moves smoothly through space horizontal to the ground: in the same direction or in the opposite direction (countermove) of the subject. The camera may track around a subject or object in a circle (circle shot).
    Dolly shot: The camera, which is mounted on a moving support, moves freely through space horizontal to the ground. In contrast to a tracking shot, however, it is not restricted to just rectilinear movements.
    Crane shot: The camera, which is mounted on a crane, appears to move freely in space above the ground.
    Handheld shot: The camera operator simply holds the camera in his or her hands and uses his or her body as the camera support.
    Steadicam:
    The camera is attached to the camera operator’s body with a stabilizing mount that mechanically separates the operator’s movement from the camera, thus allowing very smooth shots similar to those of a dolly or tracking devices.
  3. Apparent camera movements (the camera does not actually move, but the focal length of the lens system is changed)
    Zoom in: Zooming in can focus on important details by narrowing the angle of view and simultaneously enlarging the main target of the zoom.
    Zoom out: Zooming out conveys the context of the whole action by providing a larger angle of view.
