expressionism: A style of art that uses elements of distortion and exaggeration for emotional effect, and which first surfaced in the art literature and painting of the early twentieth century. In film analysis, the term “Expressionism” is frequently used to refer to stylistic devices that either resemble or draw inspiration from the German Expressionism movement. This film movement was characterized by the use of exaggerated settings, strong contrast with dramatic cast shadows, canted angles and distorted, respectively ambiguous space. The most important examples of German Expressionistic films are Robert Wiene’s The Cabinet of Dr. Caligari (1920), The Golem: How He Came Into the World (1920), Fritz Lang’s Metropolis (1927) and F. W. Murnau’s Nosferatu, a Symphony of Horror (1922). In general, the term expressionism can be used to describe film styles of “heightened artifice”.
