formalism: In filmmaking, a particular style which favours aesthetic form over the subject matter as content. Most formalist filmmakers have no desire to show (“common sense”) reality; instead, they want to convey their individual vision of the world (or their version of reality). In formalist films, cinematic time and space are often distorted, and sets, decoration, costumes and other elements of the mise en scène may also be exaggerated. Formalist filmmakers tend to use elaborate cinematography techniques (e.g. unusual uses or special patterns of camera distance, angles, focus and lighting). At the extreme end of the scale, formalist filmmakers may avoid the concept of a story and characters as the centre of a film altogether, instead trying to convey a particular mood or emotion by showing largely abstract pictures. At a less extreme point of the scale, a film may be formalistic with regard to just one aspect of cinematography or the mise en scène. However, since the formalist style naturally draws attention to itself, the perception of a formalist film may be felt as being more difficult than for a film of the classical or realistic style (see classicism and realism). It is therefore helpful to think of a film as being more or less formalistic along a scale with the further reference points of realism (at the opposite end) and classicism (in the middle between realistic and formalistic).
