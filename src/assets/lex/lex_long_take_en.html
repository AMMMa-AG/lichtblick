long take: A long take is, first and foremost, a shot of long duration. The term should not be confused with “take”, which is one of the many recordings of a given shot, or the term “long shot”, which is a shot taken from a specific camera distance.
A long take is, by definition, uninterrupted (see shot) and lasts much longer than is conventional, either for the editing pace of the film itself or of films in general. The average shot length (ASL) of classical Hollywood films is about 9 seconds, and most contemporary films (particularly those of the action genre) tend to have faster editing rates and thus a significantly lower ASL, so in general, any shot above 40 seconds can clearly be considered a long take.

Long takes can be used for dramatic and narrative effect as an alternative to a series of shots or even a complete scene, but typically require a great deal of planning and organization. When an entire scene is rendered in one single shot, this long take is called a sequence shot. Given that the duration of a shot and the filmed events usually seem to be roughly equal, a long take can have the advantage of appearing to be more realistic and documentary-style than an edited scene composed of several short shots.

The long take, usually in combination with complex camera movement, deep focus and deep space has become part of the signature style of many well-known directors (including Orson Welles, Paul Thomas Anderson, Robert Altman and Martin Scorsese).
