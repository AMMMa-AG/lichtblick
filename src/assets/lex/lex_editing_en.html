editing: The process of selecting, trimming, arranging, and joining together the separate camera takes of exposed footage into a single filmstrip after shooting has been completed. The process of “editing a film” is often alternatively called “cutting a film”.
The straight cut is the simplest form of editing but there are many other possible ways to transition from one shot to another.

In addition, editing can enable far more than just the connecting of different shots. Editing is a valuable tool at the filmmaker’s disposal, which allows the creation of a certain order and rhythm of the action as well as the modification of space and time by working with the spatial and temporal relations between the connected shots. Flashback and flash-forward edits can construct jumps backwards or forwards in diegetic time, while cross-cutting editing and parallel editing can relate several lines of action in time. Time may also appear expanded (see slow motion, freeze frame, overlapping action) or condensed (see fast motion, elliptical editing, montage).

Above all, editing is an important part of cinematic storytelling, because it is during this process that the duration of shots is finally determined and those shots are then combined to scenes and sequences. In considering the significance of this aspect, it is important to realize that it is the succession of the edited shots that creates meaning for the viewers: a gaze shot combined with a point-of-view shot lets us assume what a character is looking at; a match cut may relate completely different things through space and time; a jump cut may give us the impression of disturbance, and a reaction shot constructs a cause-and-effect relationship between two shots.

When implemented skilfully, the system of continuity editing, with its rules (e.g. 180-degree and 30-degree rule, eyeline match) and conventions (e.g. cutting on action, shot/reverse shot), greatly helps to create and maintain the illusion of the continuous and seemingly uninterrupted flow of action in a story.
