point-of-view shot (POV): A shot taken with the camera placed approximately where at least one of the character’s eyes would be, with the effect that the viewers temporarily share the assumed view of a character or group. This kind of shot is generally used to encourage, or sometimes even to force, the viewers to identify emotionally or intellectually with a character.

A POV shot is actually accomplished through a combination of shots, which are usually cut in before or after a shot of the character looking in a particular direction. In this respect, it can be seen that POV cutting is a type of eyeline match. Nevertheless, while eyeline matches and POV shots are similar in both showing the film viewer the object of the character’s gaze, a POV cut differs from an eyeline match in showing the object strictly from the perspective of the character; eyeline matches, in contrast, can show more space around the object and can also be shot from a slightly different angle.

In the case of a POV shot in which the camera is moving, it is very important that the camera movement matches the natural movement of the relevant character, e.g. imagine a POV shot of a running dog.

Further cinematographic aspects that have to be carefully matched in order to achieve plausible point-of-view shots are camera distance and camera angle.
