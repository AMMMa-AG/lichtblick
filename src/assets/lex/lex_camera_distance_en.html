camera distance: Camera distance is a classification system for shots and determines the amount of the subject matter that is visible in the frame. In particular, it helps to determine how large a subject will appear relative to the setting within the frame, and which level of details will be observable. Although the name “camera distance” suggests that the camera is closer to or further away from the subject, the term is actually related only to the “apparent” distance between the camera lens and the filmed subject (for example, the camera appears close in a close-up). The term “apparent” needs to be stressed, because the use of different lens types (and hence different focal lengths) means that the perceived distance does not necessarily correspond with the real distance. The system of different camera distances is related to psychological theories of closeness and distance between subjects, called proxemic patterns.

One of the most commonly used camera distance designation systems is related to the scale of the human body and differentiates close-up (CU), medium shot (MS) and long shot (LS) framings, with the close-up being used for close distance showing a part of the body, the medium shot for medium distance showing half of the body and the long shot for long distance showing the full body. The close-up framing can be further divided into extreme close-up (ECU) and medium close-up (MCU) framings. The same applies to the long shot, resulting in the extreme long shot (ELS) and the medium long shot (MLS). For ease of use, this system of camera distances has adopted a standard set of abbreviations for each of the different shot types.
